# The Gates

The eight points where a day of intended work either turns into shipped work or
quietly leaks away. The rituals run the stretches between them. The gates are
where you decide, and where you stay honest. This is the operating reference —
the terse, runnable form. The narrative version with worked examples lives in the
field guide.

Each gate names its **mode** — Commit (deciding what deserves you), Reckon
(facing what you did), or Pattern (reading the trend) — so you know what kind of
call you're making. You do not need to already be disciplined to use this. Each
gate tells you how to work it even when it's the one you naturally skip.

**Run Gate ∞ first, every day.** It decides how much of the rest today earns. A
day with one exhausted hour earns a one-line log. A normal day earns morning,
reckon, and plan. A Sunday earns the weekly pass.

---

## Gate ∞ — How much of this loop does today deserve?

_Mode: above the others. The master gate._

- **Decide:** Right-size the loop to the day you actually have, not the day you
  wish you had. Full ritual on a dead day makes the system a tax you'll quit. No
  reckoning on a slipping week is how avoidance goes unseen.
- **Good judgment:** Real working time → run morning and reckon at minimum, that's
  where the honesty lives. Survival day → log one line so the thread isn't lost.
  Never confuse "I performed the ritual" with "I moved the thing."
- **If this is your weak spot:** This is the one gate you can't hand to the system —
  it's the decision about how much to trust the system. Unsure? Do the reckoning.
  It's the cheapest gate and the one that keeps you honest.
- **Failure modes:** Full ceremony every day until it feels pointless and you quit.
  Skipping the loop on hard days, which are the days it's for. Doing the ceremony
  and mistaking it for the work.

## Gate 0 — What am I trying to move?

_Mode: Commit. Set when goals change, not daily._

- **Decide:** Name the small set of outcomes worth your scarce attention, and be
  willing to leave a tempting goal off. Every goal you keep is attention taken
  from the others.
- **Good judgment:** The goal is an outcome, not an activity ("ship improvements
  customers notice," not "learn four tools"). Write your known avoidance patterns
  next to the goals — that's what lets the later gates catch them. Keep the list
  short enough that "the one thing today" is a real choice.
- **If this is your weak spot:** Force a ranking. Ask the agent to sort your goals
  by "what happens if this slips a month" and watch which ones you actually
  flinch at. The rest probably don't earn a slot.
- **Failure modes:** Ten goals, so every day's focus is arbitrary. Goals stated as
  activities, so you can be busy all week and move nothing. Never naming your
  avoidance patterns, so the system can't catch you repeating them.

## Gate 1 — What's the one thing today?

_Mode: Commit. Daily, at the start of the window._

- **Decide:** Name the single most important thing to move today, and make it the
  thing that serves a goal, not the thing that's easy to start. One, not five.
- **Good judgment:** Chosen against a goal, not against your mood. Pick the
  important-and-uncomfortable over the satisfying-and-safe. Two secondaries is the
  ceiling — a day in the margins fits about one real thing.
- **If this is your weak spot:** Always drawn to the easy visible task? Have the
  system put your comfortable pick next to the goal-moving one and ask which
  you'll regret skipping by Friday. Tie-breaker: the thing that, if it were the
  only thing you did all day, would still make it a good day.
- **Failure modes:** A focus list of five, so nothing is the priority. Choosing the
  satisfying task over the one that moves a goal. Skipping the morning gate and
  letting the day choose for you, badly.

---

## Gate 2 — Did I capture what I did?

_Mode: Reckon. Throughout the day, at every break._

- **Decide:** Capture it now, in one line, before it's gone. A timestamp and what
  happened — not a polished journal entry.
- **Good judgment:** Log at the moment it happens, because on the margins the idea
  at a red light is gone by the keyboard. Capture decisions *and their reasons* —
  the reason is what you can't reconstruct in three days. Keep it cheap enough to
  never be worth skipping.
- **If this is your weak spot:** Attach logging to something you already do — close
  the laptop, take the walk, switch contexts. Let the agent do the writing; you
  just say the sentence. The bar is one line.
- **Failure modes:** Real work, logged nothing, so the reckoning gate is blind and
  so are you. Saving it all for one entry you're too tired to write. Losing the
  fix or the decision because you trusted memory in a life without room for it.

---

## Gate 3 — Did I do what I said?

_Mode: Reckon. End of day — the checkpoint most systems skip._

- **Decide:** Face the gap truthfully. Did the one thing move, and if not, why,
  and does that count as a deferral you own. The system's integrity lives here.
  Lie at this gate and everything downstream is fiction.
- **Good judgment:** Mark the miss as a miss instead of editing this morning's goal
  to match what you happened to do. Let the deferral increment — the count is the
  signal. Separate "I chose not to, for a reason" from "I avoided it."
- **If this is your weak spot:** Let the system do the diff, not you, so the
  comparison is mechanical and you can't fudge it. The rule that keeps it honest:
  you don't get to change this morning's focus after the fact.
- **Failure modes:** Rewriting the morning goal at night so you always "hit" it —
  the most common way these systems quietly die. Counting a busy day as a good
  day. Skipping the reckoning because you know you'll feel bad, which is exactly
  when you need it.

## Gate 4 — Am I avoiding this?

_Mode: Pattern. Triggered when a deferral count crosses three._

- **Decide:** Kill it or commit to it. Re-commit with a real, specific reason and a
  slot, or take it off the list honestly. What you don't get to do is let it ride
  at a low simmer forever, taxing your attention daily without ever getting done.
- **Good judgment:** Treat a 3-plus deferral as information about *you*, not the
  task. Name which of your patterns this is (infrastructure over output, research
  without shipping). Be willing to delete a task you've carried a month — carrying
  it was the cost. Re-commit with a reason tomorrow-you can't wriggle out of.
- **If this is your weak spot:** Avoid the avoidance gate itself? Let the trigger do
  the forcing — it fires at three without your permission. Answer one question: if
  I've dodged this three times, is that the task's fault or mine. Then it dies
  today or it's tomorrow's primary with a reason. No third option.
- **Failure modes:** Letting the alert become wallpaper you scroll past. Re-committing
  with a vague "I'll do it" that defers it a fourth time. Never killing anything,
  so your list is a museum of good intentions that taxes you daily.

---

## Gate 5 — What's tomorrow?

_Mode: Commit. Right after the reckoning, while the day's truth is fresh._

- **Decide:** Commit to tomorrow's one thing now, so the morning is a start and not
  a negotiation. Explicitly handle any 3-plus deferral — re-commit with a slot or
  cut it.
- **Good judgment:** Set tomorrow while today is still honest, before the excuses
  form. Put the deferred-but-important thing first — the reason it's deferred is
  that it never wins a fair morning fight. Write it durably enough that a two-day
  gap doesn't erase it.
- **If this is your weak spot:** Let the system draft tomorrow from today's
  reckoning, so planning is editing, not inventing. The key move: put the plan
  where tomorrow-you will actually see it at the start of the window. In the
  margins there's no warm-up time.
- **Failure modes:** Starting every morning from zero, burning your best minutes on
  reconstruction. Letting a deferred item drift onto tomorrow's list without a
  decision, so it just resets its dodge. Planning an ambitious tomorrow that
  ignores how today actually went.

---

## Gate 6 — What patterns emerged?

_Mode: Pattern. Once a week — the view the daily gates can't give you._

- **Decide:** Read the week honestly and let it change next week. Which pattern is
  real, which goal is being starved, which avoidance is now a habit.
- **Good judgment:** Look at the alignment score without flinching, even when it
  says most of the week went to things no goal needed. Treat a run of logging gaps
  as data, not a verdict — ask what made those days fall out of the loop. Let a
  repeating deferral graduate into a named pattern.
- **If this is your weak spot:** Make it mechanical and short. Let the system
  produce the numbers so you're reacting to a report, not grading yourself from
  memory. Read three things: the alignment percentage, the worst deferral, the gap
  days. That's enough to steer the week.
- **Failure modes:** Skipping the review — the only gate that sees patterns — so you
  repeat the same month indefinitely. Reading the signals and rationalizing each
  away. Chasing a cheerful summary instead of what happened.

## Off the loop: `decide`

Not a gate — an on-demand ritual for when a single decision is circling. It runs
the deferral engine's lens on one choice instead of the whole day: reads your
goals and deferral log, names the avoidance pattern (Mirror), strips the choice to
one sentence (Real Decision), and forces one action this week (Recommendation +
Next Step). A decision you keep re-opening is the same signal as a task you keep
deferring, so `decide` can log it into the same `patterns.md` the weekly gate
reads. Reach for it between gates; the numbered loop above is unchanged.

## The state underneath

Where all this gets written down — plain markdown, or your Obsidian vault if you
keep one — is a disposable implementation detail. See
`reference/the-deferral-engine.md`. Never anchor on the files. Anchor on the
gates. When you rebuild the files next year, the gates don't change.
